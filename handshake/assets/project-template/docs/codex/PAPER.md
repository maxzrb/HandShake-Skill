## Writing Rules

- Do not invent citations.
- Mark unsupported claims as pending verification.
- Record source gaps before marking a section complete.
- If the project has a required citation style, preserve it and record unresolved bibliographic details for manual review.

## Writing Preferences

Use these preferences for paper-writing, teaching design, coursework, literature review, and other formal Chinese writing tasks.

- Prefer Simplified Chinese when communicating with the user, unless the user asks for another language.
- Write in a natural, readable, and academically appropriate style. The target level is graduate student or teacher-training student writing: standard, careful, and logically clear, but not stiff, promotional, or mechanically academic.
- Avoid obvious AI-style writing, including repetitive phrasing, inflated abstractions, excessive conceptual stacking, and overuse of rigid frameworks.
- Do not split ordinary prose into too many bullet points. Use bullets only when they improve readability.
- Avoid overusing very short sentences, dashes, quotation marks, formulaic transitions, and unnecessary analytical labels.
- For formal paper text, keep the language steady and usable. Prefer clear and natural wording over deliberately difficult or grand wording.
- References, policy documents, curriculum standards, journal information, data, DOI, URL, author names, titles, years, volumes, issues, and pages must be real and verifiable.
- Do not fabricate sources or bibliographic details. If a detail cannot be verified, mark it as `待人工复核` or explain the uncertainty directly.
- For online-checkable or time-sensitive information, such as current policies, data, journal requirements, software documentation, API documentation, model names, prices, times, and versions, verify online when available before making factual claims.
- Avoid vague closing advice. Provide content that can be directly used, revised, copied, or executed.
